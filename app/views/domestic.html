Energy Performance Certificates (EPCs)

EPCs are needed whenever a property is:

  • built
  • sold
  • rented

Property owners, landlords or letting agents must get an EPC for potential buyers and tenants before they market a property to sell or rent.

In Scotland, you must display the EPC somewhere in the property. For example, in the meter cupboard or next to the boiler.

!
Warning

You can be fined if you do not get an EPC when you need one.

What is an EPC?

An EPC contains:

  • information about a property’s energy use and typical energy costs
  • recommendations about how to reduce energy use and save money

An EPC gives a property an energy performance rating from A (most efficient) to G (least efficient) and is valid for 10 years.

Check if a property has an EPC

To see existing EPCs for a property, you can:

  • Search for EPCs in England, Wales and Northern Ireland
  • Search for EPCs in Scotland

You can opt out of the EPC register if you do not want other people to be able to see your EPC.

How to get a new EPC

You will need to find a qualified assessor to assess your property to get a new EPC. To do this, you can:

  • Find qualified assessors in England, Wales and Northern Ireland
  • Find qualified assessors in Scotland

Letting the property

Before a property is rented, it must have a valid EPC and energy rating.

Landlords of private rented property can read guidance on the minimum energy efficiency standard.

Tenants in England should read the checklist for renting.

Properties that do not need an EPC

These include:

  • places of worship
  • temporary buildings that will be used for less than 2 years
  • stand-alone buildings with total useful floor space of less than 50 square metres
  • industrial sites, workshops and non-domestic agricultural buildings that don’t use a lot of energy
  • some buildings that are due to be demolished
  • holiday accommodation that’s rented out for less than 4 months a year or is let under a licence to occupy
  • listed buildings - you should get advice from your local authority conservation officer if the work would alter the building’s character
  • residential buildings intended to be used less than 4 months a year
